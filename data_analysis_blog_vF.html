Introduction¶

In the 21st century, digitilization has taken off due to the rise of smartphones, social media, and video conferencing in the past year. Most documents are paperless and certain legal documents are even signed electronically. However, a large number of documents (both handwritten and typed) are still physical and not digital, which means corporations, non-profit organizations, and government entities need to spend a lot of time and money to digitize them. Personally, I experienced this in a previous internship.

During the summer of 2019, I (Ben Zhao) worked for a volatility hedge fund, based in Florida, to transcribe some of their legal documents with current and potential investors onto their database. While I sifted through hundreds of documents and spent countless hours scanning over the same tables to manually input numbers and names onto an Excel spreadsheet, I knew that there must have been some type of Optical Character Recognition (OCR) program that could do the job more efficiently. That's where Amazon Textract comes in.

Amazon Textract Overview¶

Amazon Textract is not just a basic OCR program that transcribes text. Rather, Textract is a machine learning service that extracts text, handwriting, and data from scanned documents. Textract helps many companies in the financial, healthcare, and public sectors to overcome the costly and tedious process of manually extracting data from any document.

Here, Textract extracts data from a table in a printed document. Then, Textract carries over the data in its original table format for easy data analysis (Amazon Textract Documentation).

Amazon Textract Customers¶

As mentioned earlier, Textract serves organizations in the financial, healthcare, and public sectors. Above, Textract helps health insurance company Anthem to extract data and classify insurance documents from healthcare providers and clients (Amazon Textract Documentation)

Problem¶

In this post, we will show you how you can use Amazon Textract to extract handwritten text from documents with high confidence scores. Additionally, we will detect the accuracy of Textract's transcription of a database of over 3,000 handwriting samples from approximately 90 participants. Ultimately, we want to answer the following question:

Do variables (i.e. age group, gender, and dominant hand) affect Textract's transcription accuracy and produce higher/lower confidence scores for the handwriting samples?

By finding a solution to this problem, we hope to uncover any surprising hidden variables and produce statistical evidence that may affect Textract's ability to extract handwritten text accurately. This is important because the presence of these biases may have major implications in the financial, healthcare, and public sectors, which are industries where Textract is currently being used in.

Hypothesis¶

Before we conduct any analysis on the given database, we hypothesize that the variables of age, gender, and dominant hand have an effect on Textract's transcription accuracy. For age, studies have shown that sensory processing abilities, like handwriting, become more impaired for humans as they age (Canadian Journal for Occupational Therapy). Additionally, for gender, studies have shown that men and women do differ in the neural basis of handwriting due to different degrees of neural and motor activation (Human Brain Mapping).

Architectural Diagram¶

Starting with data from the CSAFE Handwriting Database, the user stores the data in an Amazon AWS S3 Bucket. Then, using Amazon SageMaker, the user pulls the data from their created bucket. Using SageMaker, the user can send the data to be analyzed and returned by Amazon Textract. At this point, with results in their SageMaker instance, a user can look at their results and run tests with everything readily hosted in the AWS Cloud.

Creating Accuracy Scores and Confidence Levels (Output Variables)¶

Then, we created a function called compare_words that would compare the words that were extracted via Textract with the original set of words in the prompts. This function would produce an accuracy score that told us what percentage of words were correct.

def compare_words(results,ref='london'):
    global ll_df, woz_df, phr_df
    valid_refs = {'london','woz','phrase'}
    if ref not in valid_refs:
        raise ValueError(f"results: status must be one of {valid_refs}")
    
    df_dict = {'london':ll_df,'woz':woz_df,'phrase':phr_df}
    
    df = pd.DataFrame({'word':np.unique(results)}) 
    df['count'] = df.word.apply(results.count)
    df = pd.merge(df_dict[ref],df,on='word',how='left')
    df = df.fillna(0)
    df['difference'] = abs(df.count_x - df.count_y)
    pct_correct = 1-df.difference.sum()/df.count_x.sum()
    return pct_correct

Another output variable that we created was called avg_conf. Since Textract assigns a confidence score to different phrases within each prompt, we took the average of all the confidence scores to come up with one average confidence score for the whole writing sample. Below is the code for creating that variable. This average confidence score for each sample would be a good benchmark for Textract's confidence level of extracting the data accurately versus our the actual accuracy score we created above.

To summarize, the output variables that we created include:

  • score: accuracy score - we took each word in the writing prompts and compared it to the words in a list that Textract transcribed from the writing samples. Then, we divided accurate words by total words and derived an accuracy score for each sample, which tells us what percentage of words Textract accurately transcribed
  • avg_conf: this metric is the average confidence score of all the Textract confidence scores that the program gives to each phrase within each writing prompt

Regression¶

To analyze the effect of variables such as age, gender, and dominant hand on Textract's accuracy for the handwriting samples, we devised some multiple linear regression models (MLRM) which are specified as follows:

Model 1: $$score_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1male + u_i $$ Model 2: $$score_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1male + \beta_2right hand + u_i $$ Model 3: $$score_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1male + \beta_2right hand + \beta_3age(25-40) + \beta_4age(41-60) + \beta_5age(61+) + u_i $$

*Note: Female, left hand, and age(18-24) were chosen as our reference groups for the dummy variables gender, dominant hand, and age, respectively.

Here, we explicitly study the effect of these variables on score, which is a direct measure of Textract's accuracy. Through our modeling, we start with simply the gender dummy variable and progressively add more to see if our findings change as more differences in writers are accounted for (which can statsitically only improve the confidence of our findings).

Our null statistical hypothesis is that none of the variables in our model have a serious effect on Textract's accuracy,

To test our hypothesis, we use the F-test for statistical inference, constructed in the following way for Model 3 in particular, as it is our most comprehensive test:

$$ H_0: \beta_1 =\text{ ... }= \beta_6 = 0 $$$$H_A: H_0 \text{ is untrue} $$

Therefore, if we reject our null hypothesis at a = 0.05 we will have uncovered that at least one variable that affects Textract's transcription accuracy.

Before actually running our regression, we must first motivate a regression approach within our current data set. In other words, to show that our regression model is valid and is the best linear unbiased estimator, we must first satisfy the 5 Gauss Markov Assumptions:

  • The form is linear

Based on our previous construction of each of our models, condition 1 is satisfied because all are linear

  • The data has been randomly selected from the population

As shown below, we have randomly sampled 1,000 participant entries from the entire dataset

  • The regressors do not display perfect multicollinearity

The regression results below do not indicate that there are strong multicollinearity problems

  • The regressors are uncorrelated with the residual term

The mean of the residuals is 0

  • The variance of the residual term remains constant

We assume heteroskedasticity in the model and correct for it using a robust linear regression fitting which corrects our subsequent inference testing

With these conditions satisfied, we know that our inference approach is valid.

Model 1

Across the board we see statistically significant coefficients for gender whether it be the only variable specified, or one of several. This suggests that true differences exist in Textract's accuracy to read handwriting samples by writers of different gender identities. As we predicted that male writers would have worse handwriting that is harder to decipher, this is not totally surprising.

Model 2

When we add an individual's dominant hand as a potentially explanatory variable for score, the coefficients for gender slightly change, and handedness (as well as intercept), possess statistically significant coefficients. We can confidently state that whether an individual is left or right handed impacts Textract's accuracy in deciphering their handwriting. Further, we can see that right handedness decreases Textract's score by -.033 when our base is left handedness.

Model 3

Our final model, the most comprehensive, additionally includes age. While gender and dominant hand variables continue to be significant, the age group 25-40 is found to not be statistically significant in predicting score; at least, compared to the base group of 18-24. That said, 41-60 and 60+ did provide statistically significant coefficients when compared to 18-24.

In the final MLRM that we specified, we find that every coefficient, except for that on age(25-40) and agegroup(61+), is statistically significantly different from 0 at the 1% level. We also produce an F statistic of 16.612, which demonstrates that each of the regressors in the model are jointly statistically significantly different from 0 at the 1% level.

This means that age characteristics, as well as the gender and favored hand of a writer, statistically impact Textract's accuracy. In fact, this holds up even at a 99% confidence level! We are very confident that these factors matter. Why an individual's righthandedness leads to lower accuracy remains unanswered, but this finding in and of itself is very interesting.

Therefore, we reject our null hypothesis that $$\beta_1 =\text{ ... }= \beta_6 = 0 $$ at the 5% level, in favor of the alternative. In other words, we can be reasonably certain that the variables we have chosen affect Textract's accuracy.

Conclusion¶

Here, we have successfully demonstrated that variables such as age group, gender, and dominant hand independently, and jointly have a statistically significant effect on Textract's accuracy. Through both an ANOVA and multiple linear regression model approach, we have shown that many of the coefficients on these variables are statistically significantly different from 0 at the 1% level, highlighting that they may have an important, non-negligible effect on Textract's accuracy. Altogether, this verifies our earlier hypothesis.
